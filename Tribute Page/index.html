APJ Abdul Kalam

APJ Abdul Kalam,
Missile Man of India

About

  • Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam BR (15 October 1931 – 27 July 2015) was an Indian aerospace scientist and statesman.
  • He served as the 11th president of India from 2002 to 2007.
  • Kalam was born and raised in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, and studied physics and aerospace engineering.
  • He was known as the "Missile Man of India" for his significant contributions to India's ballistic missile and launch vehicle technology.
  • Kalam played a crucial role in India's Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998.
  • He was widely respected as the "People's President" and received numerous prestigious awards, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian honor.

Early Life and Education

  • Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam was born on 15 October 1931 in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, to a Tamil Muslim family.
  • His father, Jainulabdeen Marakayar, was a boat owner and imam of a local mosque, while his mother, Ashiamma, was a housewife.
  • Kalam was the youngest of four brothers and one sister in his family.
  • His ancestors were wealthy Marakayar traders and landowners with properties and land in coastal Tamil Nadu and Sri Lanka.
  • However, their businesses failed by the 1920s, leaving the family in poverty.
  • Despite facing financial challenges, Kalam was a bright and hardworking student with a strong interest in learning, particularly in Mathematics.
  • He graduated in physics from Saint Joseph's College, Tiruchirappalli, and later pursued aerospace engineering at Madras Institute of Technology, though narrowly missed becoming a fighter pilot in the Indian Air Force.

Career as a Scientist

  • After graduating from the Madras Institute of Technology in 1960, Kalam joined the Aeronautical Development Establishment of the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) as a scientist.
  • He initially designed a small hovercraft but felt unsatisfied with his job at DRDO.
  • Kalam later joined INCOSPAR, working under Vikram Sarabhai, and was recruited into ISRO by H. G. S. Murthy in 1969.
  • He became the project director of India's first Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III) and successfully deployed the Rohini satellite in near-earth orbit in July 1980.
  • Kalam visited NASA's Langley Research Center, Goddard Space Flight Center, and Wallops Flight Facility in the 1960s.
  • He played a significant role in developing the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) and SLV-III projects during the 1970s and 1990s.
  • Kalam was invited to witness India's first nuclear test, Smiling Buddha, representing TBRL, even though he was not involved in its development.
  • He directed projects like Project Devil and Project Valiant, which aimed to develop ballistic missiles from the technology of the successful SLV programme.
  • Kalam served as the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister and Secretary of the Defence Research and Development Organisation from July 1992 to December 1999.
  • He played an intensive political and technological role in the Pokhran-II nuclear tests and was involved in developing various missiles under the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP). Additionally, Kalam contributed to healthcare innovations like the "Kalam-Raju Stent" and the "Kalam-Raju Tablet".

Presidency

  • Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam served as the 11th President of India from 25 July 2002 to 25 July 2007.
  • He won the 2002 presidential election with an electoral vote of 922,884, defeating his opponent Lakshmi Sahgal who secured 107,366 votes.
  • The National Democratic Alliance (NDA) nominated Kalam for the post of President, with support from the Samajwadi Party and the Nationalist Congress Party.
  • Kalam became the third President of India to receive the Bharat Ratna, the country's highest civilian honor, before assuming office.
  • During his presidency, Kalam was affectionately known as the "People's President."
  • He faced criticism for his limited action on mercy petitions, having acted on only one plea out of the 21 submitted to him during his tenure.
  • Kalam expressed support for the need for a Uniform Civil Code in India during an interactive session in Chandigarh in September 2003.
  • At the end of his first term, there were discussions about the possibility of a second term, but he eventually decided not to contest the 2007 presidential election.
  • Before the expiry of the term of the 12th President, Pratibha Patil, in 2012, media reports suggested that Kalam might be nominated for a second term, and he garnered public support for his candidature.
  • However, Kalam declined to contest the 2012 presidential poll, and ultimately, Pranab Mukherjee was elected as the 13th President of India.

Post-Presidency

  • After leaving office as the President of India, Kalam held various academic positions, including visiting professorships at Indian Institute of Management Shillong, Indian Institute of Management Ahmedabad, and Indian Institute of Management Indore.
  • He was an honorary fellow of Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore, and served as the chancellor of the Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology Thiruvananthapuram.
  • Kalam was a professor of Aerospace Engineering at Anna University and also held adjunct positions at several other academic and research institutions across India.
  • In 2011, he faced criticism from civil groups for his support of the Koodankulam Nuclear Power Plant. Protesters viewed him as a pro-nuclear scientist and were unhappy with his assurances about the plant's safety features.
  • In May 2012, Kalam initiated the "What Can I Give Movement" aimed at empowering the youth of India and focusing on defeating corruption as a central theme.

Death

  • On 27 July 2015, Abdul Kalam traveled to Shillong to deliver a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management Shillong.
  • During the lecture, he experienced discomfort while climbing a flight of stairs but entered the auditorium after a brief rest.
  • Just five minutes into his lecture, Kalam collapsed and was rushed to Bethany Hospital in critical condition.
  • Despite being placed in the intensive care unit, he was confirmed dead of a sudden cardiac arrest at 7:45 p.m. IST.
  • Kalam's body was airlifted from Shillong to Guwahati in an Indian Air Force helicopter and then flown to New Delhi
  • At Palam Air Base, his body was received by the President, the Vice President, the Prime Minister, and other dignitaries.
  • The body was taken to his Delhi residence at 10 Rajaji Marg, where the public and dignitaries paid homage.
  • On 29 July, Kalam's body was flown to Madurai, received by national and state dignitaries, and taken to Rameswaram.
  • In Rameswaram, his body was displayed for the public to pay their final respects until the evening of the same day.
  • On 30 July 2015, Abdul Kalam was laid to rest at Rameswaram's Pei Karumbu Ground with full state honors, with over 350,000 people attending the last rites, including top political figures.

Awards and Honours

  • Kalam received 7 honorary doctorates from 40 universities.
  • He was honored with the Padma Bhushan in 1981 and the Padma Vibhushan in 1990 by the Government of India for his work with ISRO and DRDO.
  • In 1997, he was awarded India's highest civilian honour, the Bharat Ratna, for his contributions to scientific research and defense technology modernization.
  • In 2013, he received the Von Braun Award from the National Space Society for his leadership in a space-related project.
  • In 2012, Kalam was ranked number 2 in Outlook India's poll of the Greatest Indian.
  • Following his death, the Tamil Nadu state government declared his birthday, 15 October, as "Youth Renaissance Day."
  • The state government instituted the "Dr. A. P. J. Abdul Kalam Award," which is presented annually to individuals from Tamil Nadu for achievements in promoting scientific growth, humanities, or student welfare.
  • On his birth anniversary in 2015, the CBSE conducted an expression series with topics related to him.
  • Postage stamps commemorating Kalam were released by Prime Minister Narendra Modi at DRDO Bhawan in New Delhi on 15 October 2015.
  • A new bacterium discovered at the International Space Station was named Solibacillus kalamii in his honor.
  • Various educational and scientific institutions and locations were renamed in his memory, such as Kerala Technological University, an agricultural college in Bihar, and A P J Abdul Kalam Technological University (formerly Uttar Pradesh Technical University).
  • India's first Medical Tech Institute was named Kalam Institute of Health Technology in Visakhapatnam.
  • A P J Abdul Kalam Memorial Travancore Institute of Digestive Diseases was established in Kollam, Kerala.
  • A new academic complex at Mahatma Gandhi University in Kerala was constructed in his memory.
  • Dr. A. P. J. Abdul Kalam Science City in Patna and a science center and planetarium in Puducherry were dedicated to him. Additionally, the Fulbright-Kalam Climate Fellowship was launched between India and the US, and the Dr APJ Abdul Kalam Planetarium in Burla, Odisha, was named after him.

Writings

  • "Wings of Fire: An Autobiography" - In this book, Kalam shares his life journey from a humble background to becoming a renowned scientist and the President of India.
  • "Ignited Minds: Unleashing the Power Within India" - Kalam discusses his vision for transforming India into a developed nation through the empowerment of its youth.
  • "India 2020: A Vision for the New Millennium" - In collaboration with Y.S. Rajan, Kalam presents a blueprint for India's development and progress by the year 2020.
  • "The Guiding Light: A Selection of Quotations from My Favourite Books" - This book is a collection of Kalam's favorite quotes from various books that have influenced him.
  • "Turning Points: A Journey Through Challenges" - Kalam reflects on the turning points in his life and the lessons he learned from overcoming challenges.
  • "Transcendence: My Spiritual Experiences with Pramukh Swamiji" - Kalam shares his spiritual experiences and interactions with Pramukh Swamiji, the spiritual head of the BAPS Swaminarayan Sanstha.
  • "Envisioning an Empowered Nation: Technology for Societal Transformation" - Kalam emphasizes the role of technology in empowering society and achieving national development.
  • "You Are Born To Blossom: Take My Journey Beyond" - In this book, Kalam provides insights and guidance to young minds to achieve their full potential.
  • "Target 3 Billion" - Co-authored with Srijan Pal Singh, this book discusses ways to address the challenges faced by the underprivileged and marginalized sections of society.
  • "Thoughts for Change: We Can Do It" - Kalam shares his thoughts and ideas on bringing positive change to society and creating a better world for all.

In Popular Culture

I Am Kalam

2011: Kalam is portrayed as a positive influence on a poor but bright Rajasthani boy named Chhotu, who renames himself Kalam in honour of his idol

People's President

2016: Indian documentary feature film directed by Pankaj Vyas which covers the life of Kalam. It was produced by the Government of India's Films Division.

My Hero Kalam

2018: Indian Kannada-language biographical film by Shivu Hiremath which portrays his life from childhood to the Pokhran tests.


Mega Icons

2018-2020: Documentary television series about prominent personalities of India which aired on National Geographic, based the third episode – "APJ Abdul Kalam" – on Kalam's life and his ascendancy to India's presidency.

Rocket Boys

2022-23: Indian Hindi-language Biographical streaming television series on SonyLIV. The character of Kalam was played by Arjun Radhakrishnan.

Rocketry:The Nambi Effect

2022: Indian biographical drama film based on the life of Nambi Narayanan, a scientist at the Indian Space Research Organisation. The character of Kalam was played by Amaan.
